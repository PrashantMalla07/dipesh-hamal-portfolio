ABSTRACT
This project focuses on the  Upgrading of  water supply and distribution system  by Sano  S ahari  K hanepani  serving the areas of  Nirmalpokhari ,  Bagmara , and  Pud .  The aim is to  fulfill the water demand  of  current and projected population demands, considering future growth and consumption trends. The design process includes  site survey,  demand analysis , supply calculation, design of  suitabl e  water treatment structure before distribution .  After survey the  existing  roughing filter was not able to treat required amount of water in a day which became obstacle resulting insufficient supply of water.  The water demand in the area was approximately 720cu.m per day but the roughing filter having dimension 20m*7m*1.2m having 4 chambers in total was only able to treat 460lpd which was clearly not sufficient.  The people were forced to use other sources of water and sometimes they had to buy water.  So  to fulfill supply of remaining 260cu.m of water per day  designing of  new  roughing filter  was necessary.  We designed the roughing filter with dimensions 9m*6m*1.2m which  could filter about 310cu.m of water per day and 9m*5m*1.2m which could filter about 260cu.m of water per day .  Among two alternative designs one will be considered  ensuring efficiency, safety, and cost-effectiveness. This report provides technical details necessary for the implementation of a sustainable water  treatment  solution, contributing to  meet the daily demand of water in  Bagmara  area.
Keywords:  water supply,  demand,  distribution,  Bagmara , roughing filter, treatment  of water

1. 1  Background
A water distribution system is a part of a water supply network with components that carry
potable water from a centralized treatment plant or wells to consumers to satisfy
residential, commercial, industrial, and firefighting requirements. The purpose of the water
supply and distribution system  is  to deliver water to consumers with appropriate quality,
quantity, and pressure. A distribution system is used to describe collectively the facilities
used to supply water from the source to the point of usage. The sources from where water
can be supplied and distributed can be classified as surface water, rainwater, underground
water, and so on. In municipal water systems, water is withdrawn from the water source
and treated before it is pumped to our homes and businesses. After treatment, municipal
water systems distribute water to homes and businesses in large pipes called water mains
that are usually buried under our roads and sidewalks. With water supply and distribution
there obviously comes along a good distribution system. During water supply, the quality
of water shouldnâ€™t deteriorate. The system also should be capable of supplying water at all
intended places with sufficient pressure heads. The water supply and distribution system
decide whether the availability of water in certain areas is sufficient good or not. The
distribution pipes are generally laid below the road pavements. This project is taken up as
people are still facing many problems to get the proper supply of water in our country even
in the main city areas. The water supply system deteriorates if itâ€™s not planned or designed
properly beforehand thinking about the possible rate of population growth.

1.2  Components of Water Supply System
A water supply system consists of several key components that work together to provide potable water to consumers. These include the source of water, treatment facilities, storage reservoirs, and a distribution network. The detailed breakdown of components of water supply system is given below:
1.2. 1 .  Source:
This is the initial location where water is obtained. It can be surface water (like rivers or lakes) or groundwater (from wells).
â–ŞSurface Water:  Rivers, lakes, and reservoirs are common surface water sources. These sources are often subject to pollution and require treatment.
â–ŞGroundwater:  Aquifers, underground layers of rock that hold water, are another vital source. Wells are used to extract groundwater.
â–ŞOther Source:  Rainwater harvesting and desalination (removing salt from seawater) can also be used, though they are less common in large-scaleÂ systems.
1.2. 2. Intake structures
This structure is used to withdraw water from the source. An intake structure is a crucial component in a water supply system, acting as the interface between the water source and the convey a nce system. It's designed to safely withdraw water while preventing debris, sediment, and other undesirable materials from entering the system. Key components include inlet screens, gates, valves, and sometimes sedimentation chambers, all working together to ensure a clean and reliable water supply.
Purpose
The primary function of an intake structure is to withdraw water from a source (river, lake, reservoir, etc.) and deliver it to the treatment plant or distribution network.
Key Components
â–Ş Inlet Screens/Trash Racks: These are designed to prevent large debris like logs, branches, and trash from entering the system.
â–Ş Gates and Valves: These control the flow of water into the system and can be used for maintenance purposes.
â–Ş Sedimentation Chambers: In some cases, sedimentation chambers are included to allow heavier particles to settle out before the water is further processed.
â–Ş Conveyance System: This refers to the pipes or channels that carry the water from the intake structure to the treatment plant.
Types of Intake Structures
â–Ş River Intakes: Often involve intake towers or submerged structures to access water from rivers, which can have varying water levels.
â–Ş Lake Intakes: Can be submerged structures or intake towers, depending on the depth and characteristics of the lake.
â–Ş Reservoir Intakes: Typically located near the dam or on the reservoir slopes, with access provided by footbridges or other means.
â–Ş Canal Intakes: Simple structures located on the bankÂ ofÂ aÂ canal.
1.2. 3. Collection Chamber:
Water supplies may be drawn from a single source or several different ones. The collection chamber is built at or near the intake site to collect water from one or more sources. When the demand for water does not fulfill by a single source, multiple sources are tapped and it is provided so that it prevents the backflow of water from one source to another. The collection chamber also breaks pressure forming a new static pressure. CC also performs as segregation of heavier particles and floating materials from raw water. Inlet, outlet, overflow, washout mechanisms are kept in the chamber.
1.2. 4. Pumping Stations
These facilities use pumps to move water from lower to higher elevations or over long distances, particularly when gravity is not sufficient. In water supply systems, pumps are crucial for lifting and transporting water from sources to distribution networks. They work by creating pressure differences that drive the water flow. Centrifugal pumps, which use rotating impellers, are common for their high flow rates and suitability for various fluids. Positive displacement pumps, known for constant flow rates, are used for high-pressure, low-flow applications.
Types of Pumps
â–Ş Centrifugal Pumps: These are widely used in water supply systems due to their ability to handle a wide range of flow rates and pressures. They work by accelerating water outwards from the center using a rotating impeller.
â–Ş Positive Displacement Pumps:  These pumps deliver a fixed volume of water with each rotation or cycle, making them ideal for applications requiring precise flow control, even at high pressures.
Key Components and Function
â–Ş Impeller: The rotating component that imparts velocity and pressure to the water.
â–Ş Casing: The housing that contains the impeller and other pump parts, directing the water flow.
â–Ş Suction and Discharge: Suction pipes draw water into the pump, while discharge pipes carry the pressurized water to the distribution system.
â–Ş Valves: Control the flow and pressure of water within the system.
â–Ş Motors: Provide the mechanical energy toÂ driveÂ theÂ pump.
1.2. 5. Distribution Chamber:
To split the supply network into manageable sub-systems serving a lesser number of public tap stand-post, a distribution chamber should be provided. It also facilitates the division of flow in the distribution sub-systems. The respective flow in each sub-system will be carried by separate pipelines from each decentralized storage tank. From the hydraulic point of view, water can be distributed with reasonable accuracy by providing a distribution chamber. It breaks incoming water pressure into the atmospheric pressure forming a new static water level.
1.2. 6. Transmission Mains:
These large pipes carry water from the source or treatment plant to storage reservoirs or directly to the distribution network. Transmission mains are a critical component of a water supply system, responsible for transporting large volumes of water, either raw or treated, from the source (like a reservoir or treatment plant) to the distribution network or storage facilities. They are the largest pipes in the system and are designed to handle high flow rates over considerable distances.
Purpose
â–Ş Conveying Water: Transmission mains act as the "highways" for water, moving it from the source (e.g., a river, well, or treatment plant) to areas where it will be used.
â–Ş Raw and Treated Water: They can carry either raw, untreated water to treatment facilities or treated water to storage reservoirs or directly into the distribution system.
â–Ş Connecting Key Points: They link the water source, treatment plants, and storage facilities, ensuring a continuous and reliable water supply.
Key Characteristics
â–Ş Large Diameter Pipes: Transmission mains are typically the largest pipes in the water supply system, capable of handling significant flow rates.
â–Ş Long Distances: They often span considerable distances, conveying water over considerable lengths.
â–Ş High Pressure: Depending on the system, they can operate under high pressure, especially when pumping water to elevated storage or long distances.
â–Ş Materials: Common materials include concrete, ductile iron, steel, and GRP/GRE pipes.
â–Ş No Distribution: Transmission mains are primarily for transport and do not distribute water directly to individual consumers.
Operations and Maintenance:
Optimal Utilization:
Operations and maintenance focus on ensuring the system operates efficiently, with minimal losses and at the lowest possible cost.
Monitoring and Control:
District Metering Areas (DMAs) can be used to monitor water usage and losses within the transmission system.
Regular Inspections:
Regular inspections and maintenance are crucial to identify and address potential issues like leaks, corrosion, or damage.
System Integrity:
Maintaining the physical, hydraulic, and water quality integrity of the transmission mains is essential for a reliableÂ waterÂ supply.
1.2. 7. Interruption chamber [IC]:
A chamber provided in the transmission main to break high pressure without a float valve is called Interruption Chamber. The function of these chamber is to release high pressure or convert into atmospheric pressure forming the new static water level. A float valve is provided in BPT whereas IC is without afloat, through the function of both is the same.
1.2. 8. Treatment Plants:
Water treatment plants purify the water by removing contaminants, ensuring it meets safety standards for drinking. A water treatment plant is a crucial component of a water supply system, responsible for purifying raw water from various sources to make it safe for consumption. It employs a series of physical, chemical, and biological processes to remove impurities and contaminants. Key components include pretreatment, core treatment (filtration, sedimentation, etc.), and disinfection. Auxiliary equipment such as pumps, storage tanks, and control systems are also essential parts of the plant.
Key Components and Processes:
â–Ş Raw Water Source: This is where the water originates (e.g., river, lake, reservoir).
â–Ş Intake Structure: A mechanism to draw water from the source.
â–Ş Pre-treatment: This stage removes large debris and suspended solids. Processes may include      screening, coagulation, and flocculation.
â–Ş Coagulation: Chemicals (like alum) are added to destabilize suspended particles.
â–Ş Flocculation: Gentle mixing to clump the destabilized particles into larger, heavier flocs.
Sedimentation: The water flows into basins where heavier flocs settle to the bottom.
â–Ş Filtration: The water passes through layers of sand, gravel, or other media to remove remaining suspended solids and some microorganisms.
â–Ş Disinfection: A final step to kill any remaining harmful bacteria or viruses using chlorine, UV light, or other methods.
â–Ş Clear Water Reservoir: Treated water is stored here before distribution.
â–Ş Distribution System: Pipes, pumps, and other infrastructure that deliver the treated water to consumers.
â–Ş Sludge Handling: Systems to manage and dispose of the removed solids and sludge.
â–Ş Laboratory: Facilities for water quality testing and monitoring.
â–Ş SCADA System: Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition system for monitoring and controlling plant operations.
â–Ş Valves and Piping: Essential for controlling water flow throughout the plant.
â–Ş Chemical Storage and Dosing: For chemicals used in the treatment processes.
â–Ş Backwash System: For cleaning the filters.
â–Ş Power Supply: Essential for operating pumps, motors, and otherÂ equipment.
1.2. 9. Storage Reservoirs:
These reservoirs, which can be elevated tanks or ground-level facilities, store treated water for later distribution. Storage reservoirs are crucial components of a water supply system, serving multiple purposes like balancing fluctuating water demand, providing emergency water, and maintaining water pressure. They can be categorized as either surface reservoirs (ground or non-elevated) or elevated reservoirs, each with specific design considerations. These reservoirs store treated water for domestic, industrial, and fire-fighting purposes, ensuring a reliable and consistent water supply.
Types of Storage Reservoirs
â–Ş Surface Reservoirs:
These are typically rectangular or circular and located at ground level. They can be supplied by gravity flow or pumping, with pumping minimized to reduce costs. They often have multiple compartments to allow for maintenance and cleaning while still maintaining supply.
â–Ş Elevated Reservoirs (Tanks/Towers):
These are constructed at higher elevations, often on hills or supported by towers, to provide sufficient water pressure in the distribution system. They can be made of steel, concrete, or other materials.
â–Ş Distribution Reservoirs:
These reservoirs hold treated water and are essential for equalizing demand fluctuations, maintaining pressure, and providing emergency storage. They typically include balancing storage, breakdown storage, and fire storage.
Functions of Storage Reservoirs
â–Ş Demand Equalization:
Reservoirs store water during periods of low demand and release it during peak demand, ensuring a consistent water supply.
â–Ş Emergency Storage:
They provide a reserve of water for  fire fighting , equipment failures, or power outages, preventing interruptions to service.
â–Ş Pressure Maintenance:
Elevated reservoirs help maintain adequate water pressure in the distribution system, particularly in areas with high elevation or long distances.
â–Ş Fire Storage:
Reservoirs can be designed to provide a specific volume of water for firefighting purposes.
â–Ş System Relief Valve:
They can act as a relief valve on a system supplied by pumping, preventing pressure surges.
â–Ş Reduction in Main Size:
With adequate storage, the size of distribution mains can be reduced, leading to cost savings.
Key Considerations
â–Ş Capacity:
The storage capacity of a reservoir is determined by the demand patterns, fire flow requirements, and the need for emergency storage.
â–Ş Location:
The location of a reservoir is crucial for effective pressure management and access to the distribution system.
â–Ş Material and Construction:
The choice of materials and construction techniques will depend on the size, location, and purpose of the reservoir.
â–Ş Maintenance:
Regular cleaning and maintenance are necessary to ensure the quality and reliability of theÂ storedÂ water.

1. 5  History of water supply and distribution in Nepal
Water has always been an integral part of Nepalâ€™s socioâ€“cultural, religious, and economic fabric. Reflecting on Water Nepal: A Historical Perspective, how deeply embedded water management traditions are in the countryâ€™s history, stretching back centuries. In ancient times, during the  Kirat ,  Lichhavi , and  Malla  periods, communities developed sophisticated and sustainable water systems such as stone spouts - dhunge  dharas _, ponds, wells, and canals. These were not just engineering feats but also carried religious and social importance. Particularly during the  Malla  era, water infrastructure flourished, often funded and managed by kings, village leaders, and even commoners. Providing access to water was seen as an act of religious merit, and festivals like  Sithi  Nakha  demonstrated the strong tradition of communal cleaning and maintenance of these sources. This strong community participation made water governance inclusive and culturally significant.
The Shah and Rana periods marked a shift toward centralized efforts and the beginnings of modern water infrastructure. The introduction of piped water systems by Bir  Shumser  in the late 19th  century,.  However, it was only after 1950, with the fall of the Rana regime and the advent of democratic governance, that Nepal embarked on formal, stateâ€“led development planning. The introduction of Fiveâ€“Year Plans starting in 1956 brought structured development to the water sector, later complemented by Threeâ€“Year Interim Plans. The creation of the Department of Water Supply and Sewerage (DWSS) 1972 institutionalized national efforts, while international agencies such as UNICEF, ADB, JICA, and FINNIDA played critical roles in funding and technical support.
These planned efforts led to a significant increase in water supply and sanitation coverage, reaching 86% and 82% respectively by 2018. Water quality issues, such as arsenic contamination in the  Terai  and rural areas and bacterial pollution in urban supplies, continue to threaten public health. Functionality of systems is also a major concern, with surveys indicating that a large proportion of drinking water projects are only partially functional. Additionally, the absence of a centralized regulatory authority, insufficient technical capacity, and the impacts of climate change including drying springs and increased disaster risks pose serious threats to the sustainability of water resources.
Urbanization has brought its own complications, especially in areas like the Kathmandu Valley, where water demand has far exceeded supply. Projects like the  Melamchi  Water Supply Project were initiated to address chronic shortages, but delays and institutional weaknesses have hampered progress. The growing urban population and the rapid pace of infrastructure development require comprehensive water governance reforms, particularly in sanitation management, which still lags behind water supply in policy and implementation.
